Throughout high school and college, I've developed several mathematical models as part of class assignments and fun competitions. Though modeling might not be the first thing you think of when you hear engineering, modeling is often used as a tool to base future experiments off of, or validate design considerations.

But for me, I really just find the modeling process to be fun. It's suprisingly both an intensely creative yet scientifically rigorous process. Below, find a few of my favorite models!

  • Skills: Python, Differential Equations, Simulations

Models

Insulin & GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

A mathematical modeling designed to determine out the effectiveness of various drugs and find optimal dosing strategies for diabetes management. The model takes a pharmacokinetic pharmacodynamic approach, and is based heavily off of enzyme kinetics models such as Hill Models and Michaelis Menten Equations.

Dates: Oct 2023 - Dec 2023

Sea Lampreys

Sea Lampreys are a unique invasive species to the great lakes that decimate the apex population. Interestingly enough, this parasite is unique from many other species in that they display a dynamic sex ratio, depending on the food scarcity in their ecosystem. This model analyzes if there may be an evolutionary advantage to this dynamic sex ratio, and what effect this has on the overarching great lake ecosystem. In addition, this model provides a potential treatment plan for addressing and controlling Sea Lamprey populations.

Submitted to COMAP's Mathematical Contest in Modeling (MCM).

Date: Feb 2024

Birth Control

This project aimed to model the differences and effectiveness of two common methods of birth control: short-term contraceptive pills and long-term arm implants. Through the use of pharmacokinetic models, we determined the safety and efficacy of Nexplanon as compared to Yasmin.

Dates: Aug 2023 - Oct 2023

Electric Vehicles

Over the past few decades of human existence, global levels of carbon dioxide gas have risen to alarming levels, catching the attention of scientists around the world and prompting humans as a whole to reduce our carbon foot- print. In this model, we provide a prediction for the number of electric trucks that will be in use, as well as the infrastructure the US might need to implement to support an electric fleet of vehicles.

Submitted to Mathwork's Math Modeling Challenge (M3).

Date: Feb 2020

Digital Divide

The digital inequality divide is the diﬀerences individuals have in the access to the internet — while some are able to take advantage of the growing power of the web, others have fallen behind, including those who cannot aﬀord to access the internet as well as those unfamiliar to technology. The first step to promote greater digital access is to first understand and model how internet and bandwidth access can change over time. The following model aims to analyze the cost of internet access for individuals, predict the bandwidth needed for various typical families, and an algorithm to compute the most cost-effective infrastructure to support that bandwidth.

Submitted to Mathwork's Math Modeling Challenge (M3).

Date: Feb 2021
